The Gateway of India

The Gateway of India, located in Mumbai, Maharashtra, is one of the most iconic monuments of India. Built in 1924, it was constructed to commemorate the arrival of King George V and Queen Mary in 1911. Standing tall beside the Arabian Sea, it symbolizes both India’s colonial past and its vibrant present.

Historical Significance

  • Construction began in 1913 and was completed in 1924.
  • It was inaugurated by the Viceroy of India, Earl of Reading, in 1924.
  • The last British troops exited India through this Gateway in 1948, symbolizing the end of British rule.

Architectural Details

  • Designed by George Wittet, a Scottish architect.
  • Blends Indo-Saracenic style with Islamic influences.
  • Made of yellow basalt and reinforced concrete.
  • Stands 26 meters (85 feet) tall with exquisite latticework.

Interesting Facts

  • It is often referred to as the "Taj Mahal of Mumbai".
  • Located near the famous Taj Mahal Palace Hotel.
  • Popular starting point for ferries to Elephanta Caves.
  • Illuminated beautifully in the evenings, making it a hotspot for tourists.

Plan Your Visit

The Gateway of India is open to visitors throughout the year with no entry fee. The best time to visit is during winter (November to February) when the weather is cool and pleasant. Don’t miss the sunrise or sunset views for a breathtaking experience!

Gateway of India

Mumbai — A majestic monument & symbol of India’s colonial history

The Gateway of India is an iconic monument built in the early 20th century to commemorate the landing of King George V and Queen Mary in India (1911). It stands at Apollo Bunder, Mumbai, overlooking the Arabian Sea.

Key Facts (Point-wise)

  • Location: Apollo Bunder, Colaba, Mumbai, Maharashtra.
  • Built: Construction started in 1911; completed in 1924.
  • Architect: Designed by George Wittet.
  • Material: Yellow basalt and reinforced concrete.
  • Style: Indo-Saracenic with Gujarati elements.
  • Height: ~26 meters (approx).
  • Significance: Emblematic of Mumbai; ceremonial entrance for British Governors and Viceroys.

History

  • Commissioned in 1911 to commemorate the royal visit of King George V and Queen Mary.
  • Constructed as a ceremonial arch to represent a gateway between land and sea.
  • Has witnessed many historic events including the departure of the last British troops in 1948.

Architecture & Design

  • Indo-Saracenic architecture blended with Hindu and Muslim motifs.
  • Large central arch flanked by smaller openings and intricate latticework.
  • Use of local basalt gives the monument its characteristic warm tone.

Visitor Information

  • Best time to visit: Early morning or late afternoon to avoid midday heat.
  • Nearby: Colaba Causeway, Taj Mahal Palace, Elephanta Island ferry terminal.
  • Entry: Open public area — no entry fee for the monument itself (ferry to Elephanta is charged).
  • Tips: Keep belongings secure; avoid large crowds during festivals; enjoy a ferry ride at sunset.
