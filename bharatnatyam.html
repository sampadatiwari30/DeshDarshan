Bharatanatyam

A classical dance form from Tamil Nadu

Bharatanatyam is a revered classical dance form from the South Indian state of Tamil Nadu. Known for its grace, purity, and emotional depth, it is considered one of the oldest dance traditions in India, with roots stretching back over 2,000 years.

Origin and History

Bharatanatyam originated in the temples of Tamil Nadu as a form of devotional expression. It was traditionally performed by temple dancers called devadasis, who dedicated their lives to temple service and performed the dance as a part of religious rituals. Over time, the dance transitioned from temples to theatres, especially during the 20th century, due to efforts by artists and revivalists who aimed to preserve India's classical arts.

Meaning of the Name

The term "Bharatanatyam" is often broken down into three syllables derived from Sanskrit:

  • Bhava – expression or emotion
  • Raga – melody or musical note
  • Tala – rhythmic pattern
  • Natyam – dance

Together, these elements form the essence of Bharatanatyam, representing a holistic art that combines music, expression, and rhythm into a single powerful performance. Bharatanatyam is characterized by a fixed upper torso, bent legs (aramandi posture), intricate footwork, and a sophisticated vocabulary of hand gestures and facial expressions used to convey emotions and stories. The dance is typically structured into a repertoire known as the Margam, which includes items like Alarippu, Varnam, and Tillana.

  • Nritta: Pure dance movements, focused on rhythm and form
  • Nritya: Expressive dance, combining rhythm with emotion
  • Natya: Dramatic storytelling through dance

Costume and Makeup

Performers wear vibrant silk sarees stitched into a special costume with pleats that fan out during movements. Jewelry includes headpieces, necklaces, earrings, bangles, and waist belts, often made of gold or imitation metal. Makeup is exaggerated for stage visibility, especially around the eyes to highlight expressions. The dancer also wears ghungroos (ankle bells) to accentuate footwork.

Music and Instruments

Bharatanatyam is typically accompanied by Carnatic music, with live musicians playing instruments such as the mridangam (drum), violin, flute, and veena. A vocalist sings compositions in Tamil, Sanskrit, or Telugu, guiding the dancer through emotional and narrative expressions.

Modern Relevance

Today, Bharatanatyam is practiced by dancers around the world and is performed on global stages, festivals, and competitions. It has become a medium for both traditional religious expression and contemporary storytelling. Institutions and dance schools continue to train students in this discipline, keeping the art form alive and evolving.

Cultural Significance

Beyond performance, Bharatanatyam is a spiritual experience for both the dancer and the audience. It is considered a form of Bhakti Yoga, or devotion through art. The dance not only preserves ancient stories and rituals but also embodies a rich heritage that connects generations through culture, discipline, and divine expression.
